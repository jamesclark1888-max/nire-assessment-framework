# 01. Philosophy

This document explains the principles that shape every decision in the NIRE Assessment Framework. When a question feels ambiguous, when a scoring rule is contested, when a new region is added, this document is the reference point.

---

## The core insight

**The questionnaire is a diagnostic instrument, not a form.**

Most "investment readiness" tools fail because they treat the questionnaire as a lead-capture form. They ask surface questions, return surface scores, and produce surface feedback. Founders fill them in once, get a vague "you're 73% ready" result, and never return.

NIRE is different. The questionnaire is the product. The output is not a score, it is a set of specific, actionable gaps that, if closed, would materially change how a VC underwrites the company.

If a founder fills in NIRE and follows the recommendations, their next pitch should land measurably better. That is the test of whether the framework is working.

---

## Five guiding principles

### 1. Ask for evidence, not opinion

**Bad:** "How strong is your team?"
**Good:** "For each co-founder, list their relevant prior experience, years in the domain, and whether they have a prior working relationship with the other founders."

VCs underwrite on evidence. Opinion-based questions are easy to game and produce noisy data. Evidence-based questions force founders to surface what they actually know (or reveal what they don't).

### 2. Every question must earn its place

A founder will tolerate a 30-minute questionnaire if they believe the output is valuable. They will abandon a 10-minute one that feels like a lead magnet.

The test for every question: would a real VC, doing real underwriting, want to know this? If the answer is no, the question doesn't belong.

### 3. Standardisation is the moat

NIRE's value to VCs depends on being able to compare 50 founders apples-to-apples. That requires standardised questions, standardised metrics, and standardised scoring. Customisation feels nice but kills the comparability that makes the VC product valuable.

This means we resist the temptation to let founders write essays in place of structured answers. We provide qualitative inputs where they're appropriate, but we structure them (character limits, rubrics, anchoring examples).

### 4. Honesty over flattery

NIRE will sometimes tell founders things they don't want to hear. The scoring is calibrated against real VC thresholds, not adjusted to make founders feel good.

A pre-revenue founder asking for £2m at a £10m valuation will score poorly on round dynamics, and that's correct. The recommendation tells them why, and what they could do about it. That honesty is the trust signal that makes the product credible.

### 5. The framework is a living thing

VC criteria change. Market conditions shift. New stages emerge (the rise of "pre-pre-seed"). New regions develop their own conventions. The framework must evolve continuously, with rigour, in version-controlled steps.

Every change to a question, weighting, or benchmark must have documented rationale. We don't tweak by feel.

---

## Two tensions we manage

### Simplicity vs depth

A simple questionnaire reaches more founders. A deep questionnaire produces better data. We resolve this with **tiered questions**: founders can get value from the required tier (15-25 minutes) but get sharper results by going deeper.

The friction is voluntary, not imposed.

### Universality vs specificity

A universal questionnaire scales. A specific questionnaire is more accurate. We resolve this with **adaptive routing**: every founder sees a tailored subset based on their stage, business model, and region. The full bank is large; what any individual sees is right-sized.

---

## What NIRE is not

To be clear about scope:

- **NIRE is not a pitch deck builder.** We assess readiness; we don't produce fundraising collateral.
- **NIRE is not an introduction service.** We are the data layer beneath introductions; the matching/intro layer sits on top.
- **NIRE is not a CRM.** VCs use NIRE to filter and assess; they manage their pipeline elsewhere (until we expand into portfolio management, which is the parallel product).
- **NIRE is not a one-time test.** Founders are encouraged to retake the assessment as their company evolves. Re-assessment data is itself valuable.

---

## The product test

When in doubt about any framework decision, ask:

1. **Would a real VC pay for the output on the other side?** If not, don't include it.
2. **Would a real founder learn something actionable from this?** If not, rework it.
3. **Can we defend this question/score with evidence?** If not, document our reasoning or change it.
4. **Does this scale across regions and stages?** If not, find the universal pattern first, then localise.

These four questions are the gate every change has to pass.
